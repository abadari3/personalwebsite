Prediction of stock prices is an incredibly complex problem, which is affected by various factors such as the economy, psychology, and politics. The model proposed in Patel, Shah, Thakkar, Kotecha had its inputs as 10 technical indicators for previous stock data, and used a variety of methods to predict the closing price n days from now. We will be modifying Patel’s fusion model with a Multilayer Perceptron Model (see project proposal), rather than a single-layer Artificial Neural Network. We analyze stock behavior using many different technical indicators, and evaluate the accuracy of our model.

In the previous Midterm Update, we collected and preprocessed our data, and set up our SVM in order to estimate technical indicator values. We use an SVR-RBF model in order to fit and predict future indicator values. We also showed the preliminary results of trying to predict stock prices using an SVR. In this Final Update, we organize our preprocessed data with technical indicators, design, train, and test our Multilayer Perceptron Model, and evaluate our performance compared to the SVR.

Due to space constraints, this final update will focus solely on the results of the MLP Model. For details about the data preprocessing and the preliminary results of the SVR model, view the Midterm Update.

Given previous a stock's market data, we wish to predict that stock’s prices over the next business week.

We will be using previous stock market data and finding the appropriate technical indicators for the previous 30-60 days, and run them through a Support Vector Regression (SVR) model to predict the technical indicators over the next 5 days. These predicted technical indicator values are then inputted to a Multilayer Perceptron Model, which predicts the closing stock price on the next five days.


We gathered stock data, consisting of daily Open, Close, High, Low, and Volume values, in the form of a pandas dataframe. In the previous midterm update, we organized our data in the form of numpy arrays, but we found that pandas dataframes are an easier way to interpret the data visually, while still working with any ML model. For information about how we calculate our technical indicators, refer to the Midterm Update.

Additionally, we had previously used matplotlib to generate market charts as images. However, due to some further research, we found Bokeh to be much more useful in making custom market data visualizations. They also have the additional benefit of being interactive.



This time, instead of keeping the days where we don't have any calculated value for the technical indicator(), we removed the first 33 business days of data from our data range. This allows us to have full data for every day in our dataset. Since we are aiming for about 50 business days of fully labelled data, our initial range of market dates to process should be between 4-5 months.

The 10 technical indicators, along with Volume gave our processed dataset 13 features. The Close column is our label data for training and validation. You may notice that the getdf() method returns more than 13 features, this is so that we can visualize the stock data with Bokeh.

In order for our preprocessing to be compatible with Bokeh and pandas, we rewrote our Stock class (stock.py), which returns a fully formatted dataframe by the .getdf() method.


Another change we made was in how we predicted technical indicator values n days into the future. Last time, we found that the SVR predictions were only valid until a maximum of 5 days into the future, after that they level off. This time, we instead trained the SVR and predicted only one day into the future at a time. This allowed us to predict further into the future, and the predictions were more accurate. Our MLP Model is based off the ideas proposed in Patel et al. However, there are a number of aspects we are changing. The MLP model is where our most substantial changes were made, including the redesign of the hidden layers, the rescaling of the features, and changing the goal of prediction to predict the daily close price loss/gain rather than the close price itself.

Most of the changes that we made in our approach are due to an obstacle that we encountered.

Our first major obstacle was that in trying to predict the SVR values for the technical indicators in the future, our model quickly fell apart after 5 days. However, as we saw in the midterm update, we saw that the SVR was fairly successful at predicting the immediate next-day values. Thus, we predicted future values by training and predicting the next day recursively. This also ensures that the predictions for the next n days will be consistent with the predictions for the next n+1 days, which was not necessarily true as we saw in our Midterm Update.


Another change that we made was changing the gamma values for each of the SVR's used in predicting the future technical indicator values. We used the same values as outlined in the paper by Patel, whose model was the inspiration for our own MLP model. This was done in order to help the performance of the SVR model, and it makes sense that different technical indicators have different levels of fitting in their prediction models. View the Midterm Update in order to see how gamma values affect the SVR.


The original Artificial Neural Network as described in Patel's paper has a single hidden layer, which two perceptrons and a bias perceptron. Additionally, the other papers referred to in our Project Proposal stated that an MLP model with many hidden layers have better results. In our first attempt at creating our MLP Model, we used our two MLP models as described above, where our training data was our formatted dataframe, our test data was the predicted technical indicator values. Our training and test labels were the true close prices for each day.

In our construction of our MLP Model, we considered two setups, one with 6 hidden layers and one with 4 hidden layers. In our first model, we had hidden layers of sizes 256, 512, 1024, 512, 256, 128. This model captures a lot of non-linear variation in our data, and should train effectively in less epochs. However, we wondered if this many layers may result in overfitting, so we also considered a secondary construction of our MLP. In our second model, we had layers of sizes 70, 28, 14, 7. Here, we would need more epochs to train our model, but each layer of the model reduces the number of dimensions of the data.

In both of our models, our activation function was rectified linear unit (ReLU). We considered using activation functions such as sigmoid or tanh, but we found the best performance with ReLU. In terms of optimizers, we used the Adam Optimizer, such that mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) is minimized. Adam Optimizer consistently converged faster than the other standard optimizers, and we chose to minimize MAPE as that is going to be our primary evaluation metric.

After our initial setup of our MLPs, we made some changes as we encountered obstacles during the process.

Our first obstacle was that our MLP would predict all future stock prices as 0, having our MAPE be stuck at 100%. The first major change we made was to rescale all of the technical indicators using MinMaxScaler() in the sklearn preprocessing library, in order to normalize the technical indicator values to improve performance of our MLP Model. This allowed our MLP to make more accurate predictions, instead of consistently undershooting.

Another big change we encountered was that the MLP predictions would have a very high variance over the next 5 days, when realistically a stock shouldn't swing so much during a single business week. This makes sense looking at the kind of data we were giving our MLP, stock close data, which consistently is on an upward trajectory. If a stock goes from 200 to 300, then we want our predictions to be biased more towards 300 and not 200. We addressed this by having the MLP predict daily price changes rather than the close price itself. This has the benefit of the model having less variance as the stock close price consistently doesn't have large changes on any given day.

Our primary metric for evaluation will be MAPE, or Mean Absolute Percentage Error, which is calculated by taking the mean of the absolute value of the percent errors between the close prices and our predicted values. We also will be referring to Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), and Mean Absolute Error (MAE) to compare our quantitative results between models.

Our backtesting procedure is to use our model to predict the close prices for every business week of 2019, and taking the average MAPE, RMSE, and MAE to evaluate the effectiveness of the model. We chose 2019 instead of 2020, as theres no way our model could have predicted the 2020 March crash due to Coronavirus concerns. Such data would only further confuse our model, so the relatively stable 2019 was chosen instead.

There were a lot of parameters we had to consider for our model, from the C and gamma values for each of our SVR models, to the many different parameters that affect our MLP.


Initially, we created each of our SVR-RBF models with the default values for C and gamma as 1000 and 0.1 respectively. However, when looking through the Patel paper, we found that our model performed better when we used the given gamma values for each of the technical indicators. We didn't change our values for C, although this could be something to pursue in future research.

For more information on how we measured the effectiveness of the SVR model, why we are using an RBF model, and the effects of C and gamma on SVRs, refer to our previous Midterm Update.

We are exploring the predictive nature of our SVR (support vector regression) and MLP (multilayer perceptron) approach in efforts to better determine a specific stock’s price given technical indicators. Ideally, this will reduce the risk of investing and offer an accurate estimate of a stock’s performance to the benefit of investors. Our approach takes the technical indicators described in the method section for a stock on day (n) and applies them to our RBF modelled SVR to predict the technical indicators on a determined day (n + t). We feed these predicted indicators into our MLP which uses weights for each inputted value to output a predicted end of day stock price for day (n + t). Since our approach disregards the importance of news reports and media coverage, our algorithm will fail to predict changes related to a company’s media presence. To improve our approach, accounting for the semantics of articles written about the company we are predicting stock prices for would be a great way to prevent consumer bias from becoming an external variable.

Our fundamental conclusion is that this avenue of trading strategy - using an SVR/MLP hybrid model - is viable. While our mean absolute percentage error was 2.966%, our model was more accurate than a typical Naive Bayes model. So, while we wouldn’t execute trades on this model, there are several things we could explore to potentially improve our accuracy. In future iterations, it would make sense to explore further hyperparameter tuning for the SVR and MLP, dimensionality reduction, and principal component analysis, as these would improve performance.
